Children’s health are increasingly at risk due to fossil fuel combustion.

Human Health | Environment


By Isabel Rowbotham, Co-Editor-in-Chief

Published March 18, 2021

Children are increasingly at risk of prematurely dying from respiratory infections. A recent study from the University of Birmingham, University of Leicester and Harvard University estimated that 7.2% of respiratory deaths in children under the age of 5 can be attributed to PM2.5 related fossil fuel burning. Children's brain, lungs and immune system are very susceptible during their early development and can become inpaired by exposure to air pollutants.


Previous studies have identified PM2.5 as a source of air pollution. PM2.5 is the airborne particulate matter derived from burning coal, petrol and diesel. The study assessed the health impact of fossil fuel burning using GEOS-Chem, a satellite simulator to estimate the number of related premature deaths due to global exposure of PM2.5 from data collected in 2012.



Fossil fuels have been historically identified as contributors to diseases and deaths. According to research by the WHO, exposure to air pollution at low levels is a major cause of global diseases. In 2015, exposure to PM2.5 caused 4.2% death of total deaths; 59% of these affecting Asia alone. Although, PM2.5 derived from fossil burning is a major contributor to air pollution, there are other non-man made sources, these include dust and wildfire smoke. In the present study, the PM2.5 concentration exposure levels were calculated exclusively from industry derived sources, such as those from oil and gas extraction.


The study looked into the effect PM2.5 pollution in children below 5 yo developing respiratory infections such as pneumonia and bronchitis. This is important as children are more susceptible to air pollution. The study estimated an average of 7.2% of respiratory mortality in children under the age of 5.


Additionally, in Europe it was identified as the largest proportion of child mortality related to fossil fuel pollution by 13.6%.



In 2012, India and China were shown to be the biggest contributors to PM2.5 in the atmosphere. North America and Europe were also shown to be contributors, the latter was shown to contribute to Africa north coastline pollution. This fossil-fuel combustion pollution was observed to contribute to excess mortality over the regions previously mentioned. The study estimated that globally 10.2 million premature deaths annually are attributable to fossil-fuel combustion, which would be 21.5% of deaths.


The solution to this problem is clearly the reduction and over-reliance on fossil fuels due to the demonstrated impact to global deaths. Fossil fuel burning is not the only source of pollution which affects human health, but is far more controllable compared to wildfire smoke sources.


The data in this study reflects global fossil fuel combustion in 2012, since then China has applied measures to decrease fossil fuel emissions. These reductions led to a 30–50% decline in PM2.5 from 2013 to 2018, which has led to the reduction of mortality from 10.2 million to 8.7 million.


It is evident that government policies aimed at reducing fossil fuel combustion have a real life impact in the reduction of premature deaths. This evidence shows the benefits in investing in greener and alternative energy sources.
